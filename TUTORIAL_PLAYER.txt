RANGE POKER TRAINER — HOW TO PLAY
==================================


THE CORE IDEA
-------------

In this game, you never know your own cards. Instead, you play an entire range of
possible hands at once. Each turn, you look at a grid of starting hands, select
groups of them, and assign a poker action — fold, call, check, bet, or raise —
to each group. The game uses your actual dealt cards (hidden from you) to determine
which action you really took, then eliminates the hands that are inconsistent with
that action. Over the course of the hand, both players' ranges get narrower and
narrower, until the showdown reveals who actually had the better cards.

The goal is to train yourself to think in ranges rather than reacting to one hand
at a time. A good player asks "what should my entire range of hands do here?" —
this game puts that question front and center on every single decision.


GETTING STARTED
---------------

On the setup screen, pick a game mode from the dropdown at the top. The standard
starting point is No Limit Hold'em. You can set the starting stack size, how many
seconds each player gets per turn, and both players' names. There is also a
"Range EV Split" checkbox — if you turn this on, the pot at showdown is split
proportionally to each player's range equity rather than going entirely to whoever
happened to be dealt the better hand. This is useful for longer study sessions
where you want to remove luck from the results.

Click Start Game when you're ready.


THE SCREEN
----------

Once the game starts, you'll see two player panels, one on each side of the screen.
Each panel has a hole card display at the top (face-down until showdown), the
player's name and chip stack, a countdown timer bar, and below that, the hand chart.

The hand chart is the main thing you interact with. It's a grid where each cell
represents a category of starting hands. The diagonal running from top-left to
bottom-right holds the pocket pairs (AA, KK, QQ, and so on down to 22). The
upper-right triangle holds the suited hands (AKs, AQs, etc.) and the lower-left
triangle holds the offsuit hands (AKo, AQo, etc.). Each cell is subdivided into
small colored rectangles representing individual suit combinations within that
hand type. Colors tell you what action has been assigned: red is fold, blue is
call or check, orange is bet or raise, and dark grey means unassigned (which
defaults to fold if facing a bet, or check if not).

In the center of the screen you'll see the community cards dealt face-up on the
board, and the current pot size. When it's your turn to act, an action panel
appears in the center.


TAKING YOUR TURN
----------------

When it's your turn, do the following:

First, select hands on your chart. You can click and drag across cells to paint
them (Freehand mode), or drag a rectangle to grab a whole region at once. Clicking
a selected cell deselects it. You can also use the Hand Types panel — expand it
with the arrow button — to select entire categories of hands at once, like "Top
Pair" or "Flush Draw." This is usually the fastest way to build a well-structured
range.

Second, click an action button to assign that action to your selected hands. Then
select a different group of hands and assign a different action. You can go back
and forth as much as you like. If you make a mistake, hit Undo to revert your
last assignment.

Third, when you're satisfied with how you've split up your range, click Submit.
The game figures out which category your actual hole cards fall into, executes
that action, and then eliminates all the other hands from your range — the ones
that took different actions. From that point forward, your range only contains the
hands consistent with what you did.

You have a limited amount of time per turn (shown by the timer bar). If you want
to save your progress partway through in case time runs out, hit Save Draft. If the
timer expires, your most recent draft is submitted automatically.


USEFUL TOOLS
------------

FOLD EARLY — If you know you'll always fold certain hands no matter what (say,
the weakest offsuit trash hands), you can select them and click Fold Early before
you start assigning. This permanently removes them from your range and clears them
off the chart so they don't clutter your view.

RANDOMIZE — Expand the Randomize panel to automatically split your range by
percentages. Set how much to call, raise, and fold (or bet and check), then click
Randomize and the game distributes those proportions randomly across your hands.
Good for quickly generating a mixed strategy.

SUIT FILTER — Lets you restrict your assignment to specific suit combinations
within a hand type. For example, you can select all AK combos but only assign
the action to the suited ones by filtering for suited. Less important for beginners
but useful for precise range construction.

STRENGTH BAR — Postflop, you can switch the center panel to "Strength Bar" view.
This shows a bar chart of all possible hands sorted weakest to strongest, colored
by their assigned action. A balanced range has folds, calls, and raises spread
across the hand strength spectrum rather than bunched together. Click the bar to expand
it into a larger popup.

RANGE SLOTS — On the far left and right of the screen, each player has five slots
for saving preflop ranges. If you develop a preflop opening range you like, save
it to a slot and load it at the start of future hands. Only available preflop.


THE SHOWDOWN
------------

When the hand ends — either someone folds, or all streets are played out — the
showdown overlay appears. Both players' hole cards are revealed, the winner is
announced, and the pot is awarded. Click Next Hand to keep playing.


---------------------------------------------------------------------
GAME MODES
---------------------------------------------------------------------


NO LIMIT HOLD'EM
----------------

The standard mode. Both players get two face-down hole cards from a standard
52-card deck. The chart has 169 hand types across a 13x13 grid, representing
all 1326 possible starting hand combinations.

Everything described above applies here. This is the best starting point if you're
new to the game.


SHORT DECK HOLD'EM
------------------

Played with a 36-card deck — all twos, threes, fours, and fives are removed.
Two rules change as a result:

  - A flush beats a full house (flushes are harder to make)
  - The wheel straight is A-6-7-8-9 (since 2-3-4-5 no longer exist)

The chart shrinks to a 9x9 grid with only the nine ranks from Six up to Ace,
representing 630 possible combos. Everything else plays the same as NLHE. The
Hand Types panel and strength bar reflect the altered hand rankings, so you'll
see Flush listed above Full House in those panels.


ACES CRACKED
------------

A special variant where the big blind is always dealt pocket aces — specifically
the Ace of Spades and Ace of Hearts — and they are dealt face-up, visible to
both players from the start of the hand.

Because of this:
  - The small blind's range automatically excludes all hands containing an ace
  - The big blind's chart shows only a single cell (the AA cell), since that
    is their only possible hand
  - The big blind cannot raise preflop — they can only call or fold. If the
    small blind limps in, the big blind may also check.

Postflop, both players act normally on their respective ranges.

This mode explores an unusual dynamic: how aggressively should the small blind
play when they know exactly what their opponent holds? And how does the big
blind maximize value postflop when their opponent can't fold them out preflop?


HOLLYWOOD
---------

Each player gets one face-down card and one face-up card. The face-up cards are
visible to both players for the entire hand.

The chart is still the standard 13x13 grid, but it's automatically filtered to
show only the hands that include your face-up card. If your face-up card is the
King of Hearts, your chart only shows combinations like KK, KQ, KJ, AK, and so
on — one card of every combo must be the K♥.

Your opponent's face-up card is shown on their side of the screen at all times.
This partial information changes the dynamic significantly: your opponent knows
one of your cards, and you know one of theirs. Bluffing is harder because your
opponent can already narrow down what you might be holding.

Hand strength is evaluated normally — your two hole cards (one face-down, one
face-up) plus up to five community cards.


HALF HOLLYWOOD
--------------

Also one face-down card plus one face-up card, but the chart works completely
differently.

Instead of a 13x13 grid of hand pairs, the chart is a 4x13 grid where each cell
represents a single specific card. The four rows are the four suits (spades,
hearts, diamonds, clubs) and the thirteen columns are the ranks from 2 up to Ace.
Every cell on the chart is one possible face-down card you could be holding.

Some cells are blacked out — these are cards that are already visible on the
table (either dealt face-up to a player, or already on the community board). You
can't be holding a card that's already accounted for, so those cells are
automatically removed from your range.

When you select cells and assign actions, you're assigning based on which specific
hidden card you might have. The game pairs each possible face-down card with your
face-up card to determine the hand strength — so the Hand Types panel and strength
bar treat the two cards together as a normal two-card hold'em hand.

There is no suit filter in this mode (the grid is already broken down by suit),
and range slots are not available.

This mode is the most granular — instead of thinking about hand types like "AKs,"
you're thinking about individual cards like "the seven of clubs."
